A trained neural model in deployment fails along three irreducible axes. Its representation may become invalid under distribution shift, drifting out of the semantic region the model actually learned. Its computation may become unstable under numerical, structural, or compilation-induced perturbations, silently amplifying tiny errors across layers. Its decision may become untrustworthy through miscalibration, overconfident output, or premature inference. A model is reliable only when all three hold simultaneously. Failing any one already breaks the system, no matter how clean the other two look.

This gives the program a precise definition:
Structural Reliability of Neural Representations  =  Representation Validity  +  Computation Stability  +  Decision Trustworthiness.
In one line: valid representations, stable computations, trustworthy decisions.

My research is the systematic execution of this decomposition. Each of the three terms above is a self-contained research question with its own core object, its own failure modes, and its own diagnostic tools; taken together, they exhaust what it means for a trained representation to be reliable in deployment.

  • Representation Validity: when does a learned representation remain in a semantically meaningful region of feature space? (OOD detection, feature-norm anomalies, gradient sensitivity, graph-spectral signals.)
  • Computation Stability: when do neural computations amplify small perturbations into large representational errors? (Graph message passing, differentiable matrix functions, finite-precision arithmetic, compiled kernels.)
  • Decision Trustworthiness: when can the output, confidence, or stopping decision of a model actually be trusted? (Calibration under quantization, bias correction, risk-controlled inference, semantic convergence.)

Within this framework I analyze failures through three technical lenses: geometry (feature distributions, local gradient flow, hidden-state trajectories), spectrum (eigenvalues, Laplacians, covariance structure), and numerics (finite precision, conditioning, compilation). A defining methodological choice across all of my work is to keep every correction post-hoc, lightweight, and theoretically grounded: no retraining, clean integration into existing pipelines, and a mathematical account of why it works.

When does a learned representation remain in a semantically valid region of feature space? Out-of-distribution detection, feature-norm anomalies, gradient sensitivity, and graph-spectral signals all measure the same underlying object: the radial, angular, and structural relationship between a representation and the region the model was actually trained on.

★  Bridge between Pillars II and III  |  Computation Stability → Decision Trustworthiness
When neural computation converges to a semantically meaningful state, the resulting decision can be trusted to stop early. This work connects the two pillars: a stability-side convergence criterion becomes a trustworthiness-side stopping rule for efficient inference, yielding sample-wise effective computation depth in large language models.
Detecting the Semantic Fixed Point: A Geometric Framework for Efficient Inference
Jiawei Gu, Ziyue Qiao, Xiao Luo
International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2026, (Oral, acceptance rate 2.6%)
Detecting semantic-state convergence as a geometric criterion for sample-wise effective inference depth: a trustworthy stopping rule for efficient inference.

Pillar III  ·  Decision Trustworthiness
When can the output, confidence, or stopping decision of a deployed model actually be trusted? Even valid representations transported through stable computation can fail at this final stage: confidence may be miscalibrated under quantization, predictions may carry systematic bias, and inference may stop too early or run too long. This pillar addresses model calibration, bias correction, and risk-controlled inference.

Jiawei Gu, Fengyuan Nie, Hao Tang, Yanpeng Sun
International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2026
Finite-precision distortion of logit margins as the root cause of confidence miscalibration in quantized networks.

NeuBM: Mitigating Model Bias in Graph Neural Networks Through Neutral Input Calibration
Jiawei Gu, Ziyue Qiao, Xiao Luo
International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence (IJCAI), 2025, (CORE A)
Mitigating systematic prediction bias in GNNs through neutral-input calibration.

Fourier Clouds: Fast Bias Correction for Imbalanced Semi-Supervised Learning
Jiawei Gu, Yidi Wang, Qingqiang Sun, Xinming Li, Xiao Luo, Ziyue Qiao

Ongoing Directions

I am extending each pillar from a set of diagnostic methods toward a unified theory.

  • Pillar I · Representation Validity. A unified geometric account that recovers norm-based, energy-based, distance-based, gradient-based, and graph-spectral OOD signals as projections of a single object, with class-conditional structure estimated from a calibration set alone.
  • Pillar II · Computation Stability. A perturbation theory of neural computation that ties graph structure, operator conditioning, and numerical precision into a single stability framework, covering both message-passing networks and differentiable matrix-function layers.
  • Pillar III · Decision Trustworthiness. A calibration and inference theory that connects finite-precision arithmetic, semantic-state convergence, and risk-controlled decision making into a unified account of when model outputs can be trusted.
